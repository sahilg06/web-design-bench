Approach — A working method

The Discipline of Restraint

Our practice sits at the intersection of research, drawing, and construction. We work slowly on purpose; each project is a proposition about how a specific place could feel to live inside.

Forma Studio was founded on the conviction that architecture is a public act performed at private scale. Every door we draw is a decision about how a stranger will meet a room; every column, a small negotiation between weight and light. We do not begin with form. We begin with the site, its geology and its habits, and with the client — a family, an institution, a city — whose life will pass through the building long after the drawings are archived. From that patient beginning we develop a set of rules: proportional, material, thermal. The building is what remains once the rules have been followed to their conclusion.

Restraint is not a style. It is the discipline of removing what a project can survive without, and it is the hardest discipline we know. A room that has been reduced to its necessities has room, in turn, for the life it was made to hold. We prize the ordinary — the well-hung door, the sun on a plaster wall at ten in the morning, the quiet click of a well-cut stair — because the ordinary is what people live inside. Extraordinary buildings, in our experience, are the ones in which the ordinary has been protected with unusual care.

We build for climate, for community, and for time. Materials are chosen for how they will age rather than how they photograph; details are drawn to survive maintenance; masterplans are structured to accept the futures we cannot yet imagine. A building, if we have done our work, will outlive its architects and its first owners and remain useful, and beautiful, in ways we did not anticipate.

The Four Phases

A method, not a formula
01
Research

We arrive with questions before we arrive with drawings. Site, program, brief, history, and climate are surveyed as one continuous field.

Deliverables include: solar and wind studies, a documented site history, precedent atlas, first-conversations transcript with the client and neighbours, and a written brief the client can sign against. Typical duration: eight to twelve weeks.

02
Concept

A single organising idea is drawn at three scales — plan, section, and detail — and tested against the constraints uncovered in research.

The concept is not a shape. It is a rule: how the building meets the ground, how the light enters the plan, how the material is intended to age. From that rule the shape follows. This phase typically runs twelve weeks and closes with a client sign-off model at 1:100.

03
Development

The concept is developed into a coordinated technical proposition, worked through with structural, environmental and cost consultants in parallel.

Full BIM coordination begins here. Every material is mocked up at 1:1 where budget allows; every detail is drawn at 1:5 minimum. Cost, carbon, and construction sequence are tested in tandem. Typical duration: six to nine months.

04
Realization

A senior architect is on site every week of construction. The drawings are not the building — the building is the building.

We work with a small register of trusted contractors and specialist trades. Site meetings are minuted and shared with the client. Post-occupancy visits are scheduled at three, twelve and thirty-six months to learn what the building has taught us.

Sustainability — Six Principles

Adopted 2019, revised 2024
Principle 01

Reuse before Renewal

The lowest-carbon building is the one already standing. We survey every site for what can remain — structure, foundations, memory — before we propose what should be added.

Principle 02

Passive First

Orientation, mass, aperture and shading do most of the work of environmental performance. Mechanical systems are asked to make up only the remainder.

Principle 03

Regional Materials

Materials are specified for what a two-hundred kilometre radius can supply. Local supply chains reduce embodied carbon and re-embed a building in its landscape.

Principle 04

Design for Disassembly

Connections are drawn to be undone. A building whose parts can be taken apart is a building whose future is not yet written.

Principle 05

Whole-Life Carbon

We measure the carbon of a project across its full expected life — construction, operation, maintenance, and end-of-life — and publish the result to the client and, where permitted, the public.

Principle 06

Water, Wildlife, Soil

Every site is a habitat before it is a plot. Water is retained, native planting is prioritised, and topsoil is preserved as if it were structural — because, for the ecosystem, it is.

Materials Palette

Standing catalogue — 2026 edition
Lime Plaster

Hand-applied, three coats. Ages toward a pale warm grey; breathes with the wall.

Portland Stone

Dorset, England. Roman-set base courses, dry-laid where possible; weathers to a soft white.

Board-Marked Concrete

Cast against rough-sawn spruce. A material record of the day it was poured.

European Oak

Rift-sawn, oiled monthly for a year, then annually. A floor that will outlast two roofs.

Slate

Cornish or Cumbrian. Split, not sawn. For paving, roofing, and sills near ground.

Hand-Made Brick

Fired in coal kilns; each brick a slightly different colour. Laid in flemish bond, lime mortar.

Blackened Steel

Hot-rolled, waxed. For frames, staircases, and thresholds that need to disappear against shadow.

Zinc Standing Seam

Pre-weathered. A roof material that will not require attention for eighty years.

Technology

BIM & Parametric Design

Our studio has worked in fully coordinated BIM since 2015; every project of ours since 2019 has been delivered LOD 400. But models are means, not ends. We use parametric tooling to interrogate a design — solar geometry, structural spans, cost-per-unit — and then to draw with more confidence at the scale of a hand.

In-house we maintain a library of custom scripts for solar-aperture optimisation, façade panelisation, and carbon accounting. Where a client wishes, we can share a live model throughout construction, so that the finished building is the drawing everyone signed off on — nothing more, nothing less.
